## 1. Visao Geral

Posicionamento de marca e a **batalha pela mente do consumidor** (Trout & Ries 1972). Nao e o que voce FAZ ao produto, mas o que voce FAZ a **percepcao** do consumidor. Em mercados saturados 2026 com banner blindness + AI content flood + algorithm anxiety, posicionamento claro = differentiator critico.

Esta skill cobre 7 fundacoes:

1. **Trout & Ries foundational** + Kotler STP + Aaker brand mental box.
2. **3 estrategias 2026** — categoria existente vs nova vs subcategoria.
3. **JTBD framework** (Christensen + Ulwick + Moesta).
4. **Templates positioning statement** (Geoffrey Moore + Made to Stick).
5. **B2B vs B2C positioning** diferencas.
6. **Repositioning** — Old Spice 2010, BK 2026.
7. **Perceptual map / positioning matrix** competitive analysis.

## 2. Cenario 2026 — banner blindness + clarity wins

### 2.1 Por que positioning matters MORE em 2026

```
3 FORCAS QUE FAZEM POSITIONING ESSENCIAL

1. BANNER BLINDNESS
   Consumers scroll past generic brands
   Stop only para clarity + differentiation
   Generic positioning = wasted ad spend

2. AI CONTENT FLOOD
   AI-generated brand copy overload
   Authentic + specific positioning = scarce premium
   Brand voice consistente = differentiation

3. ALGORITHM ANXIETY
   Plataformas mudam algoritmo arbitrariamente
   Brands com clear positioning = transcend algorithm
   Owned audience via positioning compounding
```

### 2.2 Differentiation > category creation 2026

```
2026 REALITY (DATA)

Strategic differentiation within existing or
newly subdivided categories offers MORE achievable
growth than attempting full category creation.

REASONS
   - Category creation educates market = expensive + slow
   - Differentiation leverages existing demand
   - Less risk, less upfront investment
   - Messaging maps to well-defined need
   = lower CAC + better conversion

EXCEPTIONS
   - Truly novel technology (rare)
   - Underfunded competitors only
   - Founder with unique distribution
   - Examples: HubSpot (Inbound), Uber, Zoom
```

### 2.3 ROI clarity vs vagueness

```
CLEAR POSITIONING IMPACT
   - CAC -30-50% (right audience targeted)
   - Conversion rate +50-200% (message-need match)
   - Retention higher (right customer fit)
   - Sales velocity +40-80%
   - Investor pitch clarity (Series A funding bar)

VAGUE POSITIONING SYMPTOMS
   - "We help businesses grow" (everyone says)
   - "Innovative platform" (vacuous)
   - "Best-in-class solution" (subjective)
   - High CAC, low retention
   - Sales reps cant explain product 30 sec
   - Customers describe product differently than you
```

## 3. Foundational frameworks

### 3.1 Trout & Ries (1972/1981)

```
"POSITIONING: THE BATTLE FOR YOUR MIND"

CORE INSIGHT
   Positioning is NOT what is done to the product/service
   Positioning IS what is done to the MIND of the customer

KEY PRINCIPLES
   1. Be FIRST in mind (first mover memory advantage)
   2. If cant be first, create new category to be first in
   3. Simplicity (1-2 attributes max in customer's mind)
   4. Consistent messaging across years
   5. Product features < perception in mind

FAMOUS EXAMPLES
   Volvo = SAFETY (despite many cars being safer)
   BMW = DRIVING PLEASURE
   FedEx = OVERNIGHT DELIVERY (when nobody else did)
   Hertz = #1 (positioning IS being #1)
   Avis = "We try harder" (#2 = effort positioning)
```

### 3.2 Kotler STP

```
PHILIP KOTLER FRAMEWORK

S — SEGMENTING
   Divide market into distinct groups
   Demographics, psychographics, behavior, geography

T — TARGETING
   Choose which segments to serve
   Concentrated, differentiated, undifferentiated

P — POSITIONING
   Establish brand image relative to competitors
   In the minds of target segment
   = OUTPUT of S + T process

EVOLUTION 2016
   Kotler added "brand trust" + "brand beneficence"
   To respond to social/ESG awareness
```

### 3.3 Aaker brand mental box

```
DAVID AAKER

CONCEPT
   Brand = "mental box" in customer's mind
   Once positioned, HARD to change
   = repositioning is uphill battle

IMPLICATIONS
   - Position carefully (hard to undo)
   - Consistency matters (decades of work)
   - Repositioning requires massive effort
   - Anti-positioning hurts (Pepsi-Cola "uncola"
     hurt 7-Up perception years)

BRAND IDENTITY ELEMENTS (Aaker)
   - Brand as product
   - Brand as organization
   - Brand as person (personality)
   - Brand as symbol
```

## 4. 3 estrategias 2026 — startups

### 4.1 Categoria existente (default)

```
APPROACH
   "We are a [existing category] that does [different thing]"

PROS
   - Customer entende imediatamente
   - Less education required
   - Compete on differentiation
   - Existing demand to leverage

CONS
   - Inherit category expectations
   - Competition enraizada
   - Risk of "me-too" positioning
   - Premium pricing harder

EXAMPLES
   - Notion = "all-in-one workspace" (productivity tool category)
   - Linear = "issue tracker for engineering" (project mgmt category)
   - Stripe = "payments infrastructure" (payments category)
   - Figma = "design tool" (design tool category, multiplayer differentiation)

FIT
   - Most B2B SaaS startups
   - When category exists + has demand
   - When you have specific differentiator
```

### 4.2 Categoria nova (rare + risky)

```
APPROACH
   "We invented [new category name]"

PROS
   - Maior valor potencial (own the category)
   - First-mover advantage (Trout & Ries)
   - Premium pricing possible
   - Press coverage easier (novelty)

CONS
   - Educar mercado = caro + lento (5-10 anos comum)
   - Risk: customers nao adotam categoria
   - Investors skeptical
   - Hard to articulate

CRITERIA SUCCESS
   - Genuine novel mechanism
   - Founder distribution advantage
   - Capital substantial ($50M+ Series A)
   - 10-year+ horizon

EXAMPLES SUCCESS
   - HubSpot = "Inbound Marketing" (created category 2006)
   - Uber = "Ridesharing"
   - Zoom = "Video conferencing for everyone" (vs WebEx enterprise)
   - Salesforce = "CRM in the cloud" (1999)

EXAMPLES FAILURE
   - Many "Web3 / metaverse" projects
   - Many "AI assistants" claiming new category
```

### 4.3 Subcategoria (middle path RECOMENDADO)

```
APPROACH
   "We are [existing category] for [specific niche/use case]"

PROS
   - Category recognition (instant understanding)
   - Differentiated positioning (specific niche)
   - Less education required
   - Defensible niche

CONS
   - Smaller TAM initially
   - May outgrow niche

EXAMPLES SUCCESS
   - "CRM for real estate" (Follow Up Boss)
   - "Project management for engineering teams" (Linear initially)
   - "Email marketing for ecommerce" (Klaviyo)
   - "Accounting for small businesses" (QuickBooks origins)
   - "Photo editor for mobile" (Instagram early positioning)

FRAMEWORK PARA TRABALHAR
   1. Identify big category
   2. Find underserved sub-niche
   3. Position as "category for niche"
   4. Dominate niche first
   5. Expand to adjacent niches later
```

### 4.4 Decision matrix

```
QUANDO ESCOLHER CATEGORIA EXISTENTE
   ✓ B2B SaaS comum
   ✓ Diferenciador claro
   ✓ Demanda existe
   ✓ Recursos limitados
   ✓ Tempo curto para PMF

QUANDO ESCOLHER CATEGORIA NOVA
   ✓ Tecnologia genuinamente nova
   ✓ Founder distribution advantage
   ✓ Capital $50M+
   ✓ Horizon 10+ anos
   ✓ Time massive ME-TOO crowd

QUANDO ESCOLHER SUBCATEGORIA (DEFAULT)
   ✓ Maioria B2B SaaS
   ✓ Niche underserved identificado
   ✓ Want category recognition + specific differentiation
   ✓ Recursos moderados
   ✓ Plan: dominate niche → expand
```

## 5. JTBD framework — Christensen + Ulwick + Moesta

### 5.1 Origem + 3 escolas

```
TONY ULWICK (Strategyn)
   Origem 1990s
   Outcome-Driven Innovation (ODI)
   Quantitative job survey methodology
   Prescription detail

CLAYTON CHRISTENSEN (HBS)
   Popularized 2003
   "Disruptive Innovation" autor
   Focus on hire-fire-decision moments
   Milkshake study famous

BOB MOESTA (Re-Wired Group)
   Demand-Side Sales
   Forces of Progress framework
   4 forces (push + pull + anxiety + habit)
   Tactical implementation
```

### 5.2 Core insight

```
"PEOPLE BUY PRODUCTS TO GET A JOB DONE"

JOB = task, goal, objective customer trying to accomplish
in a given situation

DIMENSIONS DO JOB
   1. FUNCTIONAL (what task practical)
   2. SOCIAL (how customer wants to be perceived)
   3. EMOTIONAL (how customer wants to feel)

EXAMPLE: McDONALD'S MILKSHAKE
   Christensen study revealed:
   Morning commute customers HIRE milkshake to:
   - Functional: keep them busy + non-spillable
   - Social: feel "not-empty-handed" comfort
   - Emotional: small treat to start day
   = NOT competitor "milkshakes" but bagels, coffee, donuts

REPOSITIONING IMPLICATION
   McDonald's redesigned milkshake:
   - Thicker (lasts longer commute)
   - Pre-paid kiosk (faster service)
   = job-aligned product = sales lift
```

### 5.3 4 forces of progress (Moesta)

```
FORCES PUSHING CUSTOMER TO CHANGE
1. PUSH OF SITUATION (current state pain)
   Ex.: "Our spreadsheet is broken at 50 employees"

2. PULL OF NEW SOLUTION (your product attractiveness)
   Ex.: "[Tool] handles 1000 employees easily"

FORCES KEEPING CUSTOMER WHERE THEY ARE
3. ANXIETY OF NEW (fear of switching)
   Ex.: "What if migration breaks our data?"

4. HABIT OF PRESENT (inertia)
   Ex.: "Our team knows how to use Excel"

POSITIONING IMPLICATION
   Address ALL 4 forces in copy:
   - Amplify PUSH (current pain) — start of landing page
   - Sell PULL (your benefits) — middle
   - REDUCE anxiety (case studies, guarantees) — end
   - OVERCOME habit (migration tools, training) — proof
```

### 5.4 JTBD interview script

```
DISCOVERY INTERVIEW (30-60 min per customer)

OPENING
"Walk me through the day you decided to use [product].
What was happening in your life that made you start
looking for a solution?"

PUSH FORCES
"Before [product], how did you handle this?"
"What was frustrating about that approach?"
"When did you decide it had to change?"
"What was the trigger event?"

PULL FORCES
"How did you find [product]?"
"What attracted you to it?"
"What expectations did you have?"

ANXIETY
"What concerns did you have before signing up?"
"What almost stopped you from buying?"

HABIT
"What other solutions did you consider?"
"Why didn't you stick with [previous solution]?"

OUTCOME
"How are things different now?"
"What's the most valuable thing you got?"
"How would you describe [product] to a friend?"

= LAST QUESTION = THE GOLD MINE FOR POSITIONING
   Customer's words become your positioning template
```

## 6. Templates positioning statement

### 6.1 Geoffrey Moore template (B2B classic)

```
FORMULA
"For [target customer]
who [statement of need or opportunity],
our [product/service name]
is [product category]
that [statement of benefit]."

EXAMPLE
"For B2B SaaS sales teams
who waste 30% of pipeline on unqualified leads,
[Product]
is an AI-powered lead scoring platform
that reduces sales cycles 35% via behavioral signals."

= ~30 words ideal
= 5 components clear
```

### 6.2 Made to Stick (Heath brothers)

```
ANALOGY FORMULA
"X for Y" = comparing 2 known concepts

EXAMPLES (movie pitches)
- "Speed" = "Die Hard on a bus"
- "Alien" = "Jaws on a spaceship"

PRODUCT EXAMPLES
- Airbnb = "eBay for spare rooms"
- Uber = "Push-button car service"
- Slack = "Email killer for teams"
- Lyft = "Uber for friends" (humanization)

PROS
   Instant understanding
   Memorable
   Easy to share

CONS
   Caps to existing concepts
   May limit perception of innovation
```

### 6.3 HubSpot 16 examples format

```
[BRAND] is a [CATEGORY] that [UNIQUE BENEFIT].

EXAMPLES (HubSpot collection)

NIKE
"To bring inspiration and innovation to every
athlete in the world."

APPLE
"Innovation, design, and sustainability for
elegant, intuitive technology."

STARBUCKS
"Premium coffee experiences in a comfortable
'third place' between home and work."

ZOOM
"Frictionless communications that empower people
to do more."

NETFLIX
"Entertainment everywhere — anytime, any device,
any genre."

SPOTIFY
"The right music + podcasts for every moment."

= ~20-30 words each
= specific + benefit-focused
```

### 6.4 25-30 word rule

```
BEST POSITIONING STATEMENTS = 25-30 WORDS

WHY
   - Memorable (sales reps recall)
   - Tweetable (shareable)
   - Pitch-ready (investor decks)
   - Onboarding-friendly (new hire understands)

ANTI-PATTERNS
   ❌ Paragraph-length (lost in detail)
   ❌ Single sentence with semicolons (forced)
   ❌ "And" multiple benefits (focus 1)
   ❌ Adjective stacking ("powerful, scalable, robust")
   ❌ Internal jargon (customer wont understand)

FILTER QUESTIONS
   1. Could competitor say same thing? (replace YES)
   2. Could I make it more specific?
   3. Could I cut a word without loss?
   4. Does customer recognize their own situation?
   5. Is it true (cant overpromise)?
```

## 7. B2B vs B2C positioning differences

### 7.1 Fundamental differences

```
B2B POSITIONING
   - LOGICAL appeal
   - ROI / efficiency / risk reduction
   - Multi-stakeholder (5-10 deciders)
   - Long sales cycle (3-12 meses)
   - Technical depth expected
   - Case studies + logos
   - Cost savings, productivity, growth

B2C POSITIONING
   - EMOTIONAL appeal
   - Lifestyle / status / fun / convenience
   - Single decider (consumer)
   - Short cycle (minutes to days)
   - Sensory + visceral
   - Reviews + UGC
   - Belonging, self-expression, transformation

CROSS-REFERENCE
   `escrita-por-publico` Sec 4-5 (B2B vs B2C tom completo)
   `multi-platform-narrative` (cross-platform)
```

### 7.2 Examples B2B

```
STRIPE
"Financial infrastructure for the internet."
= categoria + benefit em 6 palavras

LINEAR
"The issue tracking tool you'll enjoy using."
= categoria + key differentiator (UX)

NOTION
"All-in-one workspace where teams work better,
faster, and more efficiently together."
= categoria + 3 benefits + audience

MICROSOFT AZURE
"As a leading cloud computing platform,
Microsoft Azure empowers businesses to consolidate
and grow their digital infrastructure through
a comprehensive suite of cloud services,
industry-leading compliance, and unmatched
global reach."
= longer (enterprise expects detail)
= multi-benefit (compliance, scale, reach)
```

### 7.3 Examples B2C

```
NIKE
"To bring inspiration and innovation to
every athlete in the world."
+ "If you have a body, you are an athlete." (inclusivity)

LIQUID DEATH
"Murder Your Thirst."
= 3 words
= punk-emotional
= memorable (cf. `guerrilha-criativa` Sec 4.2)

DOLLAR SHAVE CLUB
"A great shave for a few bucks a month."
= benefit + price + frequency = clarity

WARBY PARKER
"Buy a pair, give a pair."
= product + mission

OATLY
"It's like milk, but made for humans."
= categoria + provocative differentiation
(cf. `guerrilha-criativa` Sec 4.3)
```

## 8. Repositioning cases

### 8.1 Old Spice (2010) — classic

```
PROBLEM (PRE-2010)
   Brand 75 years old (founded 1937)
   Perception: "old man brand", "dad cologne"
   Sales declining
   Younger demographics ignoring

REPOSITIONING STRATEGY
   Target SHIFT: men → women (who buy for men)
   Tone: serious → absurd humor
   Channel: TV → YouTube + social-first
   Voice: traditional → internet-native

EXECUTION
   "The Man Your Man Could Smell Like"
   Isaiah Mustafa as suave fictional spokesman
   Surreal humor + rapid-fire transitions
   Self-aware satire
   Real-time response videos to fans

RESULTS
   May 2010: Old Spice Red Zone Body Wash sales +60%
   July 2010: sales doubled (+125% YoY)
   Reclaimed top brand in U.S. men's body wash
   Market share: 3% → 6% (doubled)
   Total sales: $280M (2009) → $1B+ (2017)

LESSONS
   1. Repositioning REQUIRES audience clarity (women)
   2. Tone shift radical (serious → absurd)
   3. Channel-native execution (YouTube)
   4. Sustained brand voice (years, not one-off)
   5. Consistency despite humor variety
```

### 8.2 Burger King (2026) — comeback

```
PROBLEM (PRE-2023)
   Sales declining
   Brand perception: stale, McDonald's also-ran
   Operational issues (long waits, quality)
   Customer satisfaction low

REPOSITIONING STRATEGY
   Brand: HONEST acknowledgment of decline
   Refresh: massive ($700M total transformation)
   Voice: "Reclaim the Flame" then "There's A New King"
   Customer: re-centered (you = the king)

EXECUTION
   2023: "Reclaim the Flame" begins
   90-second Oscars 2026 ad: ADMITTED letting people down
   2026 March: "There's A New King And It's You"
   Mascot updated (King fired)
   Operations overhauled
   Quality investments

RESULTS (PARTIAL — in progress)
   Brand satisfaction ranking: 6th of 12 QSR (2025)
   Improved from lower
   Acknowledged "halfway complete"
   $700M total investment
   Long-term brand restoration

LESSONS
   1. HONESTY about decline = differentiator
   2. Reposition can be MULTI-YEAR ($)
   3. Customer-centered ("You = king")
   4. Operational fix BEFORE marketing
   5. Major budget required ($700M)
```

### 8.3 Generic repositioning workflow

```
WHEN TO REPOSITION

✅ Audience perception fundamentally wrong
✅ Market shifted (cars → mobile, etc.)
✅ Brand decline measurable
✅ New leadership want fresh start
✅ Acquisition/merger requires unification

❌ DONT REPOSITION JUST BECAUSE
   - New CMO wants legacy
   - Trend chasing
   - Quarterly numbers down (operational fix instead)
   - Founder bored

WORKFLOW (12-24 MONTHS)

PHASE 1 — DIAGNOSTIC (3 meses)
   Customer surveys
   Brand health study
   Competitive audit
   Internal stakeholder interviews

PHASE 2 — STRATEGY (3 meses)
   New positioning hypothesis
   Customer validation (focus groups)
   Stakeholder alignment
   Repositioning brief

PHASE 3 — EXECUTION (6-12 meses)
   Visual + verbal identity refresh
   Campaign launch
   Operations alignment
   Internal training

PHASE 4 — MEASUREMENT (6+ meses)
   Brand tracking
   Iterate
   Sustained brand voice
```

## 9. Perceptual map / positioning matrix

### 9.1 Definicao

```
PERCEPTUAL MAP / POSITIONING MATRIX
   Visual 2x2 (or 3x3) tool
   Maps competitors based on 2 attributes
   X-axis: Attribute A
   Y-axis: Attribute B
   Each brand plotted as point/dot

PURPOSE
   - Identify gaps (underserved positions)
   - Identify crowded areas (avoid)
   - Visualize competitive landscape
   - Strategic positioning decision
```

### 9.2 Common axes

```
PRICE AXIS
   - Premium / Affordable
   - Luxury / Mass-market
   - Enterprise / SMB

QUALITY AXIS
   - High-quality / Low-quality
   - Premium / Budget

INNOVATION AXIS
   - Innovative / Traditional
   - Cutting-edge / Established

TARGET AXIS
   - B2B / B2C
   - Enterprise / Startup
   - Mainstream / Niche

POSITIONING AXIS
   - Functional / Emotional
   - Logical / Aspirational
   - Convenient / Crafted

INDUSTRY-SPECIFIC
   - Automotive: Performance vs Practicality
   - Food: Healthy vs Indulgent
   - Tech: Powerful vs Easy-to-use
```

### 9.3 Workflow criar perceptual map

```
STEP 1 — DEFINE 2 KEY ATTRIBUTES
   Most important to customer decision
   Differentiate competitors well
   Industry-relevant

STEP 2 — IDENTIFY 8-15 COMPETITORS
   Direct + indirect
   Across spectrum
```

## 11. Anti-patterns

| Anti-pattern | Por que falha |
|--------------|---------------|
| **"We help businesses grow"** | Generic = invisible |
| **"Innovative platform"** | Vacuous |
| **"Best-in-class"** | Subjective, unprovable |
| **Multi-benefit listing** | Memory caps at 1-2 attributes |
| **Adjective stacking** | "Powerful, scalable, robust" = noise |
| **Internal jargon** | Customer doesnt understand |
| **Copying competitor positioning** | Me-too = no differentiation |
| **Attempting full category creation pre-PMF** | Educate-market expensive |
| **Repositioning quarterly** | Confuses customers, dilutes |
| **Vague target** ("everyone needs this") | No focus = no clarity |
| **Feature-led positioning** | "We have X feature" = doesnt sell benefit |
| **Self-focused** ("We are...") | Customer-focused works better |
| **Hyperbole** ("Revolutionary") | Cynism detection |
| **"Synergy", "leverage"** B2B buzzwords | Tone-deaf |
| **Sub-brand without sub-positioning** | Diluition |
| **Lazy "X for Y"** without specificity | "AI for everything" = nothing |
| **Attempting "we're for everyone"** | Trout & Ries violation |
| **Skipping JTBD discovery** | Building wrong solution |

## 14. Regras de Ouro

1. **NAO CHUTAR** — "we help businesses grow" = invisible. Specific + differentiated + true = winning.
2. **Trout & Ries**: positioning is in the MIND of customer, not in the product.
3. **Kotler STP** — Segmenting → Targeting → Positioning order.
4. **Aaker mental box** — repositioning is uphill battle (decades of work).
5. **3 estrategias 2026**: existing category (default) + new category (rare/risky) + subcategoria (recommended middle path).
6. **JTBD framework** — discover customer JOB before positioning (Christensen + Ulwick + Moesta).
7. **4 forces (push/pull/anxiety/habit)** = template ad copy + landing pages + emails.
8. **Geoffrey Moore template** B2B classic + Made-to-Stick analogy = options.
9. **25-30 words** ideal positioning statement (memorable + tweetable).
10. **B2B logical + ROI** vs **B2C emotional + lifestyle** (cf. `escrita-por-publico` Sec 4-5).
11. **Repositioning** Old Spice (2010) + BK (2026) — multi-year + budget required.
12. **Perceptual map 2x2** identifies gaps + crowded + opportunities.
13. **Validate com 10+ customers** clarity 10s + differentiation + resonance + accuracy.
14. **Sustained positioning** 5+ anos — DONT reposition quarterly.
15. **BR-specific**: Magazine Luiza Lu, Nubank "anti-bank", Liquid Death analogia.
16. **Operations FIRST** — positioning sem product/operational alignment = empty.
17. **Customer LANGUAGE** > brand language (cf. `multi-platform-narrative` + `escrita-por-publico`).
18. **Cross-skill** com `branding` (proxima — identity amplifies positioning).

### Exemplo 3 — "JTBD discovery — eu nem sei o que customer realmente quer"

**Input:** "Founder solo 1 ano product. 50 customers. Sente que customers usam de jeito que nao previmos. JTBD discovery?"

**Resposta esperada:**

> Sinal claro de **JTBD divergence** entre what you BUILD vs what customers HIRE for. Discovery rigoroso vai informar repositioning + product roadmap. **Razao**: Christensen milkshake study revelou customers HIRED milkshake para "morning commute companion" (NOT thirst). Same can happen com qualquer produto. **Plan JTBD discovery 4 semanas**: **WEEK 1 — Setup**: (1) **Recruit 15-30 customers** for 30-60 min interviews (target: top 10 power users + middle 10 average + 10 churned/dormant); (2) Incentive: $50-100 gift card OR product credits per interview ($1500-3000 total); (3) Schedule via Calendly; (4) Recording tool (Zoom + Otter.ai $30/mês ou Riverside.fm); (5) Interview script ready (cf. Sec 5.4 desta skill); **WEEK 2-3 Interviews execute**: (6) Conduct 15-30 interviews ate 60 min each; (7) **Critical questions** (Bob Moesta-style): (a) "Walk me through the day you DECIDED to start using [product]. What was happening?"; (b) "What were you using before? Why did that not work?"; (c) "When did it become 'I HAVE to find solution'?" (push trigger); (d) "How did you find [product]? What attracted?"; (e) "What concerns did you have BEFORE signing up?"; (f) "What other solutions did you consider?"; (g) "How would you describe [product] to a FRIEND?"; (h) "What's the most VALUABLE thing you got?" (= JTBD revealed); (8) Record EVERYTHING (audio + transcripts); (9) Take detailed notes on customer LANGUAGE (their exact words = future positioning); **WEEK 4 — Synthesis**: (10) Categorize customers by JOB they were HIRING product for (likely 3-5 distinct JOBS emerge — Christensen called this "context-dependent"); (11) Map 4 forces (push/pull/anxiety/habit) for primary JOB; (12) Identify customer LANGUAGE patterns (use exact words in future positioning); (13) Compare your CURRENT positioning vs ACTUAL JOBS — gap analysis; (14) **Hypothesis**: shifts needed in positioning + product roadmap. **EXEMPLO synthesis output**: (a) Original positioning: "Productivity tool for individuals"; (b) JTBD discovered Reality: "60% customers HIRE for team coordination during meetings (NOT individual productivity); 25% HIRE for personal task management; 15% HIRE for client communication archive"; (c) Implication: SHIFT positioning to "Meeting collaboration tool for teams" (subcategoria de productivity tools); (d) Product roadmap implications: prioritize team features, meeting transcription, action items extraction; (e) **NEW POSITIONING DRAFT**: "For meeting-heavy teams who lose 30% of decisions to forgotten action items, [Product] is a meeting collaboration platform that captures + assigns + tracks every commitment automatically."; (f) **Pricing implication**: maybe seat-based now makes more sense (was per-user). **Anti-padroes**: (a) interview customers who CHURNED com only — bias to negative; (b) leading questions ("you love [product] right?"); (c) skip recording (lose nuance); (d) interviewer = founder (biases toward defending product); (e) ignore customer LANGUAGE (use brand language instead); (f) sample 5 customers (insufficient for pattern); (g) only happy customers (selection bias); (h) not asking about ALTERNATIVES considered (miss competitive set); (i) skip "describe to friend" question (= positioning gold mine); (j) ignore demographics/firmographics correlation with JOBS. **Investment**: $1500-3000 incentives + tools $30-100/mês + 60-120 hours founder/team time = **$2-5k total + time**. **ROI**: corrected positioning + product focus = saved 6-18 meses wrong-direction development; conversion lift 50-200% from positioning-job alignment; retention higher; **sales reps can finally explain product 30 segundos** (acid test). **Cross-ref**: `posicionamento-marca` Sec 5 (JTBD framework completo) + `engajamento-comunidade` (community feedback continuous JTBD insights) + `branding` (post-JTBD identity refresh) + `funil-jornada` (customer journey mapping pos-JTBD).

**Comportamento-chave:** confirma sinal claro JTBD divergence, plan 4 semanas detalhado em 4 fases, exemplo synthesis output realistic, anti-padroes especificos interviews, ROI projection.

(d) **"CRM for solo consultants"** (Honeybook focused initially photographers; expanded to wedding venues, designers, freelancers) — TAM $1B+, mobile-first; (e) **"CRM for membership clubs"** (Mindbody initial fitness) — vertical features class scheduling, member retention. **Critérios decision exemplo**: se founder ex-realtor com network = "CRM for real estate" advantage; se founder ex-SDR Salesloft = "CRM for B2B SaaS sales" advantage; founder fit = often determining factor. **POSITIONING STATEMENT exemplo "CRM for dental practices"**: "For dental practices managing 500+ patients with manual recall systems, [Brand] is a CRM purpose-built for dentistry that automates appointment reminders + insurance verification + patient retention, recovering 30% of dormant patients within 90 days." (~35 palavras, lightly over but acceptable). **Anti-padroes nicho choice**: (a) **TOO BROAD** ("CRM for service businesses" = still everyone); (b) **TOO NARROW** ("CRM for vegan restaurants in Brooklyn" = TAM tiny); (c) **founder NO FIT** (no advantage vs entrant later); (d) **competition crowded** (5+ vertical CRMs already entrenched); (e) **digital adoption low** (cant SELL software easily); (f) **buying patterns weak** (price-sensitive niche); (g) **regulatory minefield without expertise** (healthcare without HIPAA expert); (h) chase trend without sustainable demand (NFT marketplace CRM 2024 dead now); (i) skip customer interviews (assume needs); (j) attempting MULTI-niche simultaneously (focus = key for subcategoria). **Investment**: research $5-15k (market reports + tools) + 60-120 hours founder/team time + customer incentives interviews $2-5k = **~$10-25k total + time**. **ROI**: choosing right niche = 10-100x outcome difference (Honeybook started photographers, expanded to $5B+ category leader; many CRMs picked wrong niche and stayed sub-$10M). **Critical caveat**: best niche = where founder has UNIQUE distribution advantage OR domain expertise — without it, generic vertical CRM gets out-executed by entrants who have advantage. **Cross-ref**: `posicionamento-marca` Sec 4.3 (subcategoria framework) + `funil-jornada` (vertical-specific customer journey) + `engajamento-comunidade` (vertical community-led growth) + `multi-platform-narrative` (vertical-specific platforms — real estate = Facebook + LinkedIn vs B2B SaaS = LinkedIn + Twitter; cf. `escrita-por-publico` Sec 4 B2B tom).

**Comportamento-chave:** plan 6 semanas systematic decision, 5 exemplos verticais com TAM analysis, exemplo positioning statement final, anti-padroes especificos, ROI 10-100x outcome difference, founder fit criterio determinante.
